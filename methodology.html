Methodology

How we calculate our impact.

Two claims sit behind our impact counter: hens spared and nitrogen saved. This page shows exactly how we get to those numbers, which assumptions we make, and where the data comes from, so anyone can check our work.

Status: provisional. The nitrogen figures are being verified with the Louis Bolk Institute. We publish nothing as fact until the data holds up.

Our principles

Whole egg, not protein

Scramble uses the entire egg, white and yolk. So we compare against a whole egg, never a protein-only slice of it.

Conservative by default

Where a range exists, we pick the cautious end, so the real impact is likely larger than we claim, not smaller.

Sourced and datable

Every coefficient links to a source and a date. When better data arrives, the number changes in the open.

The functional unit

We express everything per whole egg replaced. One portion of scramble is defined as 3 whole eggs. From a weekly habit we scale up:

eggs_per_year = portions_per_week × 3 × 52
Calculation A

Hens and animal lives spared

A commercial laying hen produces roughly 300 eggs per year and is slaughtered after one laying cycle. So the eggs you no longer buy translate directly into hens no longer kept and killed. Historically, about one male layer chick is culled at hatch for every hen, which we count as a second spared life.

hens_spared   = eggs_per_year / 300
animal_lives = hens_spared × (1 + 1)
Worked example

3 portions/week → 3 × 3 × 52 = 468 eggs/year → 468 / 300 = ~1.6 hens → with the male chicks, ~3 animal lives spared per year.

Note: the Netherlands and EU are moving to in-ovo sex determination, which will end routine chick culling. We will phase the male-chick term out as that becomes standard, and label the counter accordingly.

Calculation B

Reactive nitrogen saved

This is the part we treat most carefully. We compare the reactive nitrogen (Nr) lost to air and water per whole egg against the same for its lupin-scramble equivalent, using a life-cycle approach. The egg side carries losses the lupin side simply does not have:

The egg loses N through
  • Synthetic fertiliser on feed crops
  • Imported soy, with embedded N
  • Feed conversion loss: most N fed to the hen never ends up in the egg
  • Ammonia from housing and manure
  • Nitrate leaching and nitrous oxide
Lupin instead
  • Fixes its own N from the air, so little or no fertiliser
  • No animal, so no excretion or housing losses
  • Grown at home, no imported feed
  • Leaves residual N in the soil, a credit for the next crop
N_saved_per_egg = Nr_lost(egg) − Nr_lost(lupin) + soil_credit(lupin)
N_saved_year    = eggs_per_year × N_saved_per_egg
Provisional
Working example

Using a placeholder of ~3.0 g Nr saved per whole egg, 468 eggs/year → ~1.4 kg reactive nitrogen per year. This coefficient is exactly what we are verifying with the Louis Bolk Institute before it goes public as fact.
